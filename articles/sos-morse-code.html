What is SOS in Morse Code?

Three dots (S), three dashes (O), three dots (S). In transmitted Morse code, SOS is sent as a continuous sequence without the letter-gaps that would normally separate the three letters — it is treated as a single unbreakable prosign.

Does SOS Stand for Anything?

Despite popular belief, SOS does not officially stand for anything. It is not an abbreviation for "Save Our Souls" or "Save Our Ship," though these backronyms are widely repeated. The letters were chosen purely for their simplicity and distinctiveness in Morse code — three dots, three dashes, three dots is an extremely easy pattern to recognise, even in poor signal conditions, and extremely easy to tap out even without training.

Before SOS was standardised in 1908, the British used "CQD" (a general calling signal CQ + D for distress), and the Germans used "SOE". The international Berlin Radiotelegraphic Convention settled on SOS in 1906, with it coming into full force in 1908.

How to Signal SOS

The beauty of SOS is that it can be transmitted with virtually anything that can produce an on/off signal:

  • Sound — Three short blasts, three long blasts, three short blasts on a whistle, horn, or banging on a surface.
  • Light — Flash a torch or mirror: three short flashes, three long, three short.
  • Radio — Tap out ... --- ... on any radio transmitter using its key or PTT button.
  • Visual — Spell out "SOS" in large letters on the ground using rocks, wood, or clothing visible from the air.
  • Smartphone — Many modern phones have built-in SOS emergency features.

Famous SOS Incidents

  • RMS Titanic (1912) — One of the first uses of SOS at sea. The Titanic initially sent the older "CQD" code before switching to SOS. The Carpathia responded and rescued 705 survivors.
  • SS Moro Castle (1934) — A ship fire off New Jersey; the delayed SOS distress call contributed to the high death toll and led to major reforms in maritime radio protocol.
  • Apollo 13 (1970) — While not Morse code, the sequence of short and long bursts in mission control communications drew comparisons to SOS in media coverage.
