Numbers Stations: Morse Code's Secret Life as Spy Radio

The Cold War never really ended on shortwave. You can still tune in.

🕑 9 min read SpiesCold WarShortwaveHistory

Somewhere in the Russian wilderness, a shortwave radio transmitter has been broadcasting a monotonous buzzing tone almost continuously since 1976. It transmits on 4625 kHz. Occasionally, the buzzing stops, and a voice reads a series of numbers and words in Russian. Then it goes back to buzzing.

This is UVB-76, nicknamed The Buzzer by the international community of shortwave listeners who have been monitoring it for decades. And it is just one of dozens of mysterious "numbers stations" that have used Morse code and voice to communicate with spies since the Cold War — and some of which are still active today.

What Are Numbers Stations?

Numbers stations are shortwave radio broadcasts that transmit sequences of numbers, letters, or Morse code — apparently at random, to no acknowledged audience, from unacknowledged transmitters. They have been documented since World War I. During the Cold War, every major intelligence service operated them.

The logic is elegant: a spy in the field needs only a cheap shortwave receiver to receive their instructions. The transmission is one-way — the spy never transmits, so they can't be triangulated. And if the message is encrypted with a one-time pad (a perfectly unbreakable cipher, if used correctly), the transmission is theoretically impossible to decode without the key.

"The existence of numbers stations is an open secret. We know they exist, we know roughly who runs them. We just can't decode what they're saying." — Simon Mason, author of Secret Signals: The Euronumbers Mystery

Morse Code Numbers Stations

How a Morse numbers station transmission works:
  1. Station transmits an identifying preamble (often a callsign in Morse)
  2. A 3-5 digit agent number is sent — only the intended recipient continues listening
  3. A message block is transmitted: groups of 5 Morse characters, repeated twice
  4. Station signs off with "AR SK" (end of message, silence)

The Lincolnshire Poacher: Britain's Most Famous Station

Between the 1970s and 2008, a peculiar shortwave broadcast aired on multiple frequencies. It opened with a few bars of the English folk song The Lincolnshire Poacher, then a female voice read five-digit number groups. Shortwave enthusiasts traced the signal to RAF Akrotiri in Cyprus — a British military base — strongly suggesting GCHQ (British signals intelligence) as the operator.

The station went silent in 2008 with no announcement. A near-identical station, Cherry Ripe, used an Australian folk tune and was likely operated from the same facility, possibly targeting agents in East Asia.

Are Numbers Stations Still Active?

Yes — and this surprises most people. In an age of encrypted satellite communications and secure messaging apps, analog shortwave numbers stations are still transmitting. The reasons haven't changed:

  • Shortwave radio crosses borders without permission — no ISP, no telco to subpoena
  • Reception requires only hardware, not an account or subscription
  • Traffic analysis is impossible — listening generates no log, no metadata
  • A one-time-pad encrypted Morse message is mathematically unbreakable
  • Shortwave receivers cost under $30 and are sold everywhere legitimately

The Conet Project, a 1997 compilation of recordings from numbers stations, remains one of the strangest and most fascinating documents of the Cold War. It is available in full on the Internet Archive. Listening to it — the robotic voices, the five-digit groups, the eerie folk song intros — you realise these weren't dramatic spy thrillers. They were office work. Someone, somewhere, tuned in every Tuesday at 21:00, wrote down the numbers, decoded them with their one-time pad, burned the pad page, and went about their day.

You Can Listen Right Now

Numbers stations are perfectly legal to receive. A software-defined radio (SDR) dongle — available for under $25 — plugged into a laptop running SDR# or GQRX lets you tune the entire shortwave spectrum. The website Priyom.org maintains a live schedule of known active numbers stations, including the Russian M12 and several suspected Cuban stations still active today.

You might not be able to decode what you hear. But you'll be listening to one of the last pieces of Cold War infrastructure still running — a spy's inbox, broadcast in plain air, protected by math.
