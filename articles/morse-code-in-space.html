Morse Code in Space

From the Moon's surface to interstellar space — dots and dashes went further than anyone expected.

🕑 7 min read NASASpaceApolloAmateur Radio

When people think of space communication they picture futuristic radio dishes and digital telemetry. They don't picture a telegraph operator. But Morse code has followed humanity into orbit — and in at least one case, it may have saved a crew.

The Moon Has Morse Code on It

The Apollo Lunar Surface Experiments Packages (ALSEP) left on the Moon by Apollo 12 through 17 transmitted scientific data back to Earth for years. Each package included equipment that broadcast its station identifier — in Morse code — so ground controllers could confirm which mission's instruments they were talking to.

This wasn't nostalgia. Morse identifiers are short, unambiguous, and survive signal degradation better than voice. The same logic applies to aviation VOR beacons today: the 3-letter Morse identifier is the ultimate fallback when digital readouts fail.

Apollo 13: The Morse Code Connection

"Houston, we've had a problem." — Jack Swigert, Apollo 13, April 13, 1970

The Apollo 13 oxygen tank explosion on April 13, 1970 forced the crew to abandon the Command Module and shelter in the Lunar Module Aquarius as a lifeboat. The crippled Command Module's telemetry systems were partially non-functional, forcing mission controllers to devise workarounds for monitoring the spacecraft's systems.

Among the improvised techniques used during the crisis: mission controllers requested the crew tap specific switch combinations in patterns that could be confirmed as simple on/off signals — effectively binary Morse — to verify systems that couldn't be read digitally. The principle was identical to telegraph days: when complex communication fails, fall back to the simplest possible signal.

Why simple signals survive when complex ones don't:
  • A Morse dot or dash requires only a power fluctuation to transmit
  • Binary on/off can be confirmed even through heavy interference
  • No decoding hardware needed — a trained ear is enough
  • Signal can be sent via light, radio, or physical contact equally

Amateur Radio Operators and Space

The International Space Station carries amateur radio equipment as part of the ARISS (Amateur Radio on the International Space Station) programme. Astronauts with amateur radio licences — and several have them — can and do communicate with Earth-based ham operators in their off-hours.

Morse code (called CW, for Continuous Wave, in amateur radio) is transmitted from the ISS on 145.800 MHz. The ISS also automatically broadcasts telemetry packets, and some astronauts have transmitted personal CW messages to amateur operators below — a 400km-altitude version of a telegram.

The Voyager Probes: Still Talking

Launched in 1977, Voyager 1 and 2 are now in interstellar space — the farthest human-made objects ever. They still transmit data back to Earth using a 23-watt radio signal (roughly the power of a refrigerator light bulb). By the time those signals reach NASA's Deep Space Network dishes, they are extraordinarily faint.

The encoding used — phase modulation with error correction — is conceptually descended from the same principle as Morse code: information encoded as variations in a simple signal. The engineers who designed Voyager's communication system had many of them trained on or alongside Morse-code-era telemetry. The vocabulary changed; the grammar didn't.

Morse Code in Astronaut Training

Until the 1990s, Morse code proficiency was a requirement for many NASA and Soviet space programme roles — not for transmitting messages, but because understanding low-bandwidth signal encoding was considered foundational for anyone working with spacecraft telemetry. The mental model of "encode information as time-based signal variations" is the same whether you're tapping a key or designing a deep-space transponder.

Several Apollo-era astronauts held amateur radio licences and were proficient Morse operators. Owen Garriott (Skylab 3) made the first amateur radio contact from space in 1983. His son Richard Garriott — game designer and private astronaut — later continued the tradition from the ISS in 2008.
